Biochemistry · Q1 Tap to reveal
1
Question

A child presents with muscle weakness and exercise intolerance due to inability to break down glycogen in muscle.

Which disease is this?

Biochemistry · Q1 Tap to go back
Answer
McArdle Disease
Reason
Deficiency of muscle glycogen phosphorylase.
Glycogen cannot convert to glucose-1-phosphate in muscle.
Leads to cramps and no rise in lactate after exercise.
Mnemonic
"McArdle = Muscle can't mobilize glycogen"

MCQ

The committed and rate-limiting step of glycolysis is catalyzed by:

AHexokinase
BPyruvate kinase
CPhosphofructokinase-1
DGlucose-6-phosphatase
Biochemistry · Q2 Tap to go back
Answer
C – PFK-1
Explanation
PFK-1 catalyzes the conversion of fructose-6-phosphate → fructose-1,6-bisphosphate.
It is the committed and rate-limiting step of glycolysis.
Tightly regulated by ATP, AMP, and citrate levels.

Physiology · Q1 Tap to reveal
1
Question

A patient with severe hemorrhage develops tachycardia.

Which autonomic system is responsible?

Physiology · Q1 Tap to go back
Answer
Sympathetic Nervous System
Reason
Blood loss → decreased blood pressure.
Baroreceptors detect the drop and trigger sympathetic activation.
Sympathetic stimulation increases heart rate and contractility to maintain perfusion.
Physiology · Q2 Tap to reveal
2
Question

A patient with severe vomiting develops metabolic alkalosis.

Why does vomiting cause alkalosis?

Physiology · Q2 Tap to go back
Answer
Loss of HCl
Reason
Gastric juice is rich in hydrochloric acid (HCl).
Persistent vomiting removes H⁺ ions from the body.
Loss of H⁺ raises blood bicarbonate → metabolic alkalosis.
Mnemonic
"Vomit → Lose Acid → Alkalosis."
Physiology · Q3 Tap to reveal
3
MCQ

Which of the following causes a rightward shift of the oxygen dissociation curve?

ADecreased CO₂
BIncreased pH
CDecreased temperature
DIncreased 2,3-BPG
Physiology · Q3 Tap to go back
Answer
D – Increased 2,3-BPG
Explanation
Increased 2,3-BPG decreases haemoglobin's affinity for O₂, facilitating oxygen release to tissues.
Other causes of rightward shift: ↑CO₂, ↑temperature, ↓pH (Bohr effect).
Rightward shift = decreased O₂ affinity = more O₂ delivered to tissues.

1
Question

A patient cannot extend the wrist, resulting in wrist drop after a mid-shaft humeral fracture.

Which nerve is injured?

Anatomy · Q1 Tap to go back
Answer
The Radial Nerve
Reason
Radial nerve runs in the radial groove of the humerus.
Supplies extensor muscles of the forearm.
Damage → loss of wrist/finger extension → wrist drop.
Mnemonic
"Radial = Raise the wrist." — If wrist can't raise → radial nerve injury.
Anatomy · Q2 Tap to reveal
2
Question

A patient presents with inability to abduct the arm beyond the first 15° after a shoulder injury.

Which nerve is most likely damaged?

Anatomy · Q2 Tap to go back
Answer
The Axillary Nerve
Reason
Deltoid muscle abducts the arm from 15°–90°.
Innervated by the axillary nerve (C5–C6).
Injury often follows surgical neck fractures of the humerus.
Mnemonic
"D for Deltoid, D for 90 Degrees." — Deltoid (Axillary) → abducts to 90°.

MCQ

A 70-year-old woman sustains a fracture of the neck of femur. Months later she develops avascular necrosis of the femoral head. The most likely damaged artery is:

AObturator artery
BLateral circumflex femoral artery
CMedial circumflex femoral artery
DInferior gluteal artery
Anatomy · Q4 Tap to go back
Answer
C – Medial Circumflex Femoral
Explanation
The medial circumflex femoral artery is the primary blood supply to the femoral head.
Disruption during neck of femur fracture → ischaemia → avascular necrosis.
This is why femoral neck fractures carry a high risk of AVN.

Clinical

A 55-year-old man presents with polyuria, polydipsia, and unexplained weight loss. His random plasma glucose is 14 mmol/L.

What confirms the diagnosis?

Clinical Case · Q1 Tap to go back
Answer
Diabetes Mellitus
Diagnostic Criteria — Mnemonic: "DRIP"
D – Diabetes symptoms (polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss)
R – Random glucose ≥ 11.1 mmol/L
I – Impaired fasting glucose ≥ 7.0 mmol/L
P – Post OGTT ≥ 11.1 mmol/L

A 12-year-old boy presents with recurrent episodes of sudden jerking movements and loss of awareness lasting a few seconds.

What is the basic physiological mechanism behind epilepsy?

Clinical Case B · Q2 Tap to go back
Answer
Abnormal Neuronal Discharge
Key Neurotransmitter — Mnemonic: "GI"
G – ↓ GABA (inhibitory neurotransmitter)
I – ↑ Glutamate excitation
Epilepsy: excitatory activity exceeds inhibitory control in neuronal networks.

MCQ

Virchow's triad includes all of the following EXCEPT:

AHypercoagulability
BEndothelial injury
CStasis
DThrombocytopenia
Pathology · Q1 Tap to go back
Answer
D – Thrombocytopenia
Explanation
Virchow's triad: Endothelial injury, Stasis, Hypercoagulability.
These are the three primary factors predisposing to thrombosis.
Thrombocytopenia does not promote thrombosis — it leads to bleeding instead.

Integrated Clinical · Q1 Tap to reveal
Long Case
A 55-year-old man presents to the emergency department with a 2-hour history of severe crushing retrosternal chest pain radiating to his left arm and jaw. The pain began suddenly while resting and is associated with diaphoresis, nausea, and shortness of breath.

He has a history of hypertension and hyperlipidaemia. Pulse rate is 108 bpm, BP is 145/90 mmHg. ECG shows ST-segment elevation in leads II, III, and aVF.

What is the most likely diagnosis?

AStable angina
BPulmonary embolism
CMyocardial infarction
DAortic dissection
Integrated Clinical · Q1 Tap to go back
Answer
C – Myocardial Infarction
Clinical Reasoning
→Crushing chest pain radiating to left arm/jaw = classic ischaemic cardiac pain
→Onset at rest = suggests complete coronary occlusion
→Diaphoresis + nausea = autonomic activation seen in MI
→ST elevation in II, III, aVF = inferior STEMI (RCA territory)
Risk Factors Present
Hypertension + hyperlipidaemia → atherosclerosis → plaque rupture → MI.
